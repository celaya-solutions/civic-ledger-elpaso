Dear Community,
It is my honor to present you with the New Mexico Environmental Law Center’s 2024 Impact Report!
I can say without a doubt that 2024 marks one of the most historic years for our public interestnonprofit law center and the reason why as many of you know, is because NMELC was selected out ofthousands of nonprofits across the nation to receive a one time gift of $2,000,000 from MacKenzieScott’s Yield Open Call. To date, it is the largest unrestricted gift NMELC has received in its 38 yearhistory and for that we remain eternally grateful and humbled!
Many of you have asked or wondered what we’ve done with such an amazingly generous gift. I amhappy to share that the first thing we did was invest in our own staff by providing timely cost of livingadjustments and increasing our employer match for our retirement program. We also used a portion ofthe gift to offset budgetary deficits we’d been experiencing due to funder shifts and a national declinein individual giving most nonprofits faced a few years ago. 
But, the most exciting update on how we’ve used this gift is the purchase of our own property! All of usat NMELC are proud to announce that we, after 38 years of existence, are the owners of our very ownoffice space! This once in a lifetime opportunity to purchase our own facility was only possiblebecause of the Yield Gift. Without this gift, the opportunity to transition from renting office space tohaving and owning a permanent home office would have remained an unreachable dream. This is atestament to how transformational large, unrestricted gifts can be for nonprofits everywhere. Owningour own property provides long-term stability and sustainability, asset building, and a strongcommunity presence for NMELC. 
As I share this historic news with you, the emotions well upinside because I never imagined being in this positionespecially because of the major financial challenges ourorganization has faced in the past few years.
I want to uplift and recognize all of the individuals whohelped us to achieve this major milestone because we donot accomplish such successes alone. 
I especially want to thank you for your continued supportand belief in my leadership. It is such an honor for me, aXicana from working class roots and from an environmentaljustice community, to lead such a powerful, committed,and wonderful organization.
Stay tuned for more details on our Office WarmingCelebration in December!
In solidarity,
Virginia Necochea

Action
Alongside the Mountain View Coalition (MVC),
NMELC fought for the implementation of the
Health, Equity and Environmental Impacts
(HEEI) Rule, to protect air quality and community
health in overburdened areas of Bernalillo
County. NMELC and MVC are defending the rule
against multiple appeals by industry groups and
filed a Civil Rights Complaint to the EPA against
the City of Albuquerque.
Alongside Red Water Pond Road Community, NMELC fought for the removal
of Quivira Mine uranium mine waste near Churchrock to the proposed,
engineered and lined Red Rock Landfill. Together we led a successful
campaign to persuade EPA to choose removal to the Red Rock Landfill as the
preferred option and clarified misinformation about the waste.
Alongside Empowerment Congress of Doña Ana County and Sunland
Park/Santa Teresa community members, NMELC continued to support
efforts to obtain clean and safe drinking water in Southern New Mexico,
including pushing for federal and state investigations into the Camino
Real Regional Utility Authority (CRRUA). We worked to not only protect
the fundamental right to clean and safe drinking water, but also to ensure
water remains in community hands. 
Alongside Eastern Navajo Diné Against Uranium Mining (ENDAUM) and 
Red Water Pond Road Community, NMELC brought together a multi-tribal
delegation to a historic Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (IACHR)
Thematic Hearing in Washington, D.C., on “Impacts of Uranium Exploitation on the
Human Rights of Indigenous Peoples in the United States.” Through powerful and
moving testimony, the panel confronted the U.S. government on the devastating
health, environmental and cultural impacts from the uranium industry.

NMELC worked alongside Tewa Women United (TWU) in their campaign to protect all residents
in surrounding communities—especially the most vulnerable, infants and children—urging the
New Mexico Environment Department (NMED) to deny Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL)
a permit to vent dangerous radioactive tritium into the air, which was initially postponed.
Unfortunately, on September 4, 2025, NMED authorized LANL’s project despite public
opposition and many unknowns. NMELC continues to work with TWU and community partners
to ensure transparency and accountability from NMED and LANL.

Alongside Catron County residents, NMELC knocked down yet another
illegal water grab attempt by Augustin Plains Ranch (APR), as a 
Judge denied APR’s latest request for a massive water transfer 
of 54,000 acre feet of water per year. Thousands of residents’ 
access to precious water in the high desert remains protected.

NMELC is a federally recognized 501(c)(3) tax-exempt organization.EIN #85-0360664.  
The New Mexico Environmental Law Center has
been defending environmental justice since 1987. 
It is our mission to work alongside New Mexico’s
communities to protect our air, land and water in
the fight for environmental justice.
Our Mission
NMELC is committed to dismantling the racist
structures that are at the heart of environmental
injustice and all disparate treatment of EJ
communities. If we do not respect the water we
drink, the air we breathe, the land we sow, and the
community in which we live, we cannot realize the
fundamental human rights to which we are all
entitled. We stand with those seeking justice and will
continue to utilize our platform to support our state
and its people.
